Six Years as a Software Engineer: What I Wish I Had Known

personal2026-04-03

Six years ago I wrote my first production pull request, terrified it would break something. This year I led the architecture of a system processing 400 million events per day. Looking back, most of what made the difference wasn't technical. Here's what I'd tell my earlier self.

Your Code Is Not Your Identity

The hardest lesson was learning to separate my self-worth from my pull requests. Early on, every code review comment felt like a personal attack. It took years to internalize that criticism of code is just engineering feedback — the kind that makes the product better and makes you better. Engineers who treat code review as a collaborative process rather than a performance evaluation grow much faster.

Invest in Adjacent Skills Early

Technical skills plateau without complementary skills in communication, estimation, and stakeholder management. The engineers I've watched have the biggest impact consistently are not the most technically brilliant — they're the ones who can translate complex tradeoffs into business terms, build trust with product managers, and unblock themselves by navigating organizational dynamics.

The Power of Saying "I Don't Know"

Admitting uncertainty early builds more trust than false confidence that collapses later.

Build in Public, Learn in Public

Writing about what I was learning — even when I wasn't an expert — accelerated my growth more than anything else. It forced clarity of thought and connected me with people who could point out the gaps in my understanding. Start a blog, write internal docs, speak at a local meetup. The compounding returns are real.
